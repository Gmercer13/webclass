The African Black Footed Cat

About the Black Footed Cat

The black-footed cat, also called the small-spotter cat, is the smallest wild cat in Africa and is endemic to the southwestern arid zone of Southern Africa. This cat has a lifespan of 10 years (in captivity) and can be up to 9.8 inches tall. These black-footed cats are found only in three countries of Southern Africa: Botswana, Namibia, and South Africa.

Where do they live?

Black-footed cats are nocturnal inhabitants of the arid lands of southern Africa, and are typically associated with open, sandy, grassy habitats with sparse scrub and tree cover. Their habitats seem to be savannah areas with long grass and higher rodent and bird densities. During the day, these cats live in abandoned burrows dug or in holes in termite mounds.

Conservation

The African Black-footed cat is rare compared to the other small cats of southern Africa. Some threats that these cats face are poisoned baits and traps, as well as poisoned locusts which are their preferred food. There is also a loss of grassland due to overgrazing by livestock that is prevalent throughout the species' range, which could be their biggest threat. Another big threat can be the habitat deterioration that led to reductions of the cat's small vertebrate prey base.

Fun Facts

These black-footed cats is a nocturnal animal that travels upp to 5 miles per night to find food. They can kill and eat up to 14 small animals per night. Also, these cats rarely drink water, so they absorb the moisture from their food instead. Another name for the black-footed cat is "miershooptier", also meaning "anthill tiger". This refers to the aggressive behavior that is a characteristic for the corned black-footed cats. Lastly, these black-footed cats can survive up to 13 years in the captivity.
